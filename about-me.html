About Me

What I Learned as a Small Business Owner

In 2018, I did what every middle-aged man supposedly dreams of—I opened my own pub. A friend and I brewed beer, built a brand, and launched a bar in South East London. Over six years, we brewed multiple beers, employed 36 staff, and generated over £1 million in revenue. At the end of June 2024, we served our final pint and closed the doors on what had been an unforgettable chapter. Even though I never gave up my day job, running the pub had a huge impact on me. It taught me more than I ever expected and confirmed a few things I already knew:

    1. I’m curious and love mastering new skills.
    2. I have a passion for analysing data.
    3. I’m pretty good at solving problems.

Here’s what those lessons look like in real life.


Shortly after the launch of Little Faith in 2019

Curiosity and the Drive to Learn

I’ve always been curious about how things work. After 10 years as an analyst in the property sector, I wanted to get hands-on experience in running a business. At Camden Market, I worked with over 280 small businesses, which gave me a close-up view of their challenges and successes. Later, I helped develop a hotel business made from shipping containers in Waterloo. But opening my own pub was something else entirely—it was my chance to prove I could build something from scratch.

And I did. In three weeks, I designed the bar and project-managed the conversion of a 1930s warehouse. I hired a team, set up systems, and even built a data pipeline so I could regularly track how the business was performing. On top of that, I threw myself into marketing—creating websites, managing social media, and hosting events (43 in the first year alone). It was a rush, and seeing friends enjoy what we’d created made it all worthwhile.


But over time, I realised that the day-to-day running of the business wasn’t enough for me. I missed learning new things and solving new problems. That’s when I started thinking about where my real passion lies—and it brought me back to data.


My Love for Data

It turns out running a pub isn’t just about pouring pints. The part I loved most was digging into the data and using it to make better decisions. For example:

  • Tracking Trends: I used our point-of-sale system to monitor beer wastage, creating weekly reports in Excel. Those insights helped us order smarter, cutting waste and boosting profits.
  • Cutting Costs: By analysing our banking data, I found cheaper providers for our POS system and business bank account. Switching saved us thousands over the years.
  • Streamlining Operations: I crunched the numbers on our kitchen’s performance and figured out the best times to open and close it to maximise profitability.

Those experiences made one thing clear: I love using data to solve problems and uncover opportunities. That’s the kind of work I want to focus on full-time—helping businesses use their data to make smarter decisions.


Analysing (lack of) profit of the bar for a presentation to stakeholders

Problem-Solving Under Pressure

Of course, running a bar wasn’t all spreadsheets and beer festivals. When COVID-19 hit, we were thrown into a cash flow crisis like so many others. To stay afloat, I came up with a plan to sell membership packages and pre-sell beer. It worked—we raised £13,000, which helped us reopen, pay our core staff, and clear our stock.

That’s just one example of how running a small business pushed me to think creatively and act quickly. Problem-solving became second nature, and I know that skill will serve me just as well in the world of data analytics.

What’s Next

Running the pub taught me a lot, but most importantly, it helped me figure out what I really want to do: work with data, solve problems, and keep learning.
